Read in order: (1) the theoretical core—view-based / simple maps and why MOLA adopts them; (2) how that splits into .simplemap vs. .mm; (3) modular pipelines and ICP-style pose tracking; then our two field configurations and a reproducible command sequence. Primary reference: Blanco-Claraco, “A flexible framework for accurate LiDAR odometry, map manipulation, and localization,” arXiv:2407.20465 (IJRR, 2025).

Step 1 — Core idea: view-based maps (“simple maps”)

Classical pipelines often commit early to one metric representation (e.g. a single fused point cloud) and discard intermediate structure. The MOLA line of work defends the opposite: treat a view-based map—a pose graph whose nodes carry time-stamped, minimally committed sensor observations (keyframes + raw or lightly structured views)—as the fundamental map object. From that archive you can later synthesize task-specific metric maps (dense clouds, 2D projections, occupancy-style layers, localization-centric maps) without re-running a full mapping mission in the field.

In tooling terms, that archive is what you save as a simple map (*.simplemap): poses in SE(3) linked to observations, not a single frozen geometry product. Formal definitions, figures, and large-scale experiments are in arXiv:2407.20465.

Step 2 — Session archive vs. metric product: .simplemap and .mm

Following the same distinction as in arXiv:2407.20465: the simple map is the flexible, re-processable session record; a metric map (*.mm) is a derived, often downsampled / layered geometric artifact optimized for navigation, visualization, or a particular localization backend. They answer different questions: “what did the robot observe, and where?” vs. “what geometric model should the controller consume right now?”.

Aspect .simplemap .mm (metric map)
Data Keyframes: SE(3) poses + linked raw / rich observations (e.g. scans) Geometric layers: point maps, 2D grids, voxels, etc.
Role Archive the mapping session; re-run backends or build different metric maps later Deploy for localization, planning, obstacle checks, MM Viewer
Processing Structured but “raw” relative to final geometry products Filtered / merged / layered for a specific task
  • .simplemap (simple / view-based map file): serializes the mapping session— keyframes with poses and attached observations—so you can re-run backends, change filtering, or build alternative metric maps long after acquisition.
  • .mm (metric map): a posterior geometric product, typically obtained via sm2mm (simple-map-to-metric-map) with a pipeline YAML that declares how observations are fused, filtered, and exported (e.g. for MM Viewer or LiDAR localization nodes).

Step 3 — Modular front-end pipelines and ICP-style pose tracking

The same reference framework advocates composing odometry / mapping front-ends from reusable processing blocks wired in configuration (analogous to stacking layers in deep learning), instead of hard-coding a monolithic preprocessor. Typical blocks include motion compensation (deskewing) of rotating multi-beam scans, voxel or other downsampling, spatial / self-hit filtering, and map insertion into a local map representation used for registration.

Pose estimation is driven by an ICP-like iterative loop: build point-to-point or point-to-model correspondences between the current scan and the local map, solve for the aligning rigid transform, and iterate until convergence. Practical systems add robust loss / kernels on residuals to down-weight outliers, inject motion priors (e.g. from the previous velocity estimate) to shrink the search basin, and—in the MOLA formulation emphasized in arXiv:2407.20465—tightly couple linear and angular velocity estimation with that optimizer so that improved velocity feeds back into better deskewing on the next frame. (Our Scenario B additionally fuses an IMU in the full stack; the paper discusses strong LiDAR-only behavior as well.)

Scan-to-map alignment. Operationally, new scans are registered against a growing local map, not only against the previous sweep—this stabilizes tracking when frame-to-frame geometry is weak. Registration often uses GICP-class or surface-aware objectives so structured environments contribute informative constraints. Block diagrams and optimizer details are in arXiv:2407.20465.

Experiment-specific preprocessing. In our demos, ground filtering and resolution are engineering knobs on that front-end: suppressing dominant ground planes can sharpen constraints for ICP but removes floor structure from the displayed cloud; full-resolution + IMU changes the motion model and point statistics. Both remain instances of the same MOLA philosophy above.

Step 4 — Two scenarios we implemented (same site)

The following are empirical instantiations of the same MOLA stack on RoboSense ROS 2 bags: identical venue, different front-end preprocessing and sensor fusion choices, so the effect on the saved .simplemap / exported .mm is visible rather than anecdotal.

Scenario A — Ground-filtered, downsampled LiDAR (no IMU on this path). Cleaner above-ground emphasis; less floor-dominated clutter for interpretation and registration.

Scenario B — Full-resolution LiDAR + IMU fusion; ground filtering off. Denser surfaces and full ground returns; richer but visually busier.

Compare the paired images and the two YouTube links above—same workflow class, different knobs.

Step 5 — End-to-end workflow (what to run, in order)

  1. Record or obtain a ROS 2 bag with RoboSense PointCloud2; for Scenario B, include time-synchronized Imu (e.g. /imu/data).
  2. Preprocess (optional). Scenario A: downsample + ground filter before / within your launch pipeline; Scenario B: full resolution, no ground filter.
  3. Run MOLA LiDAR odometry (e.g. mola_lidar_odometry / mola-cli with your YAML) to estimate trajectory and accumulate keyframes.
  4. Save a simple map — output *.simplemap (session archive for reprocessing).
  5. Optional — build a metric map: run sm2mm with a pipeline YAML to produce *.mm for MM Viewer or localization stacks.
  6. Optional — evaluate trajectory: export keyframe poses to TUM and plot (e.g. with evo): 
pip3 install evo
sm-cli export-keyframes /path/to/map.simplemap --output kfs.tum
evo_traj tum kfs.tum -p --plot_mode=xy

Step 6 — Why it matters

The split between .simplemap and .mm is how professional MOLA workflows stay flexible: you keep one truthful session record, then mint metric products tailored to navigation or visualization. Showing two scenarios on one site makes the impact of preprocessing and IMU tangible for anyone evaluating map quality or deploying autonomy.
