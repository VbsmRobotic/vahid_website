Project overview

This project documents a repeatable offline mapping workflow: ROS 2 bag recordings are turned into consistent, metric-scale 3D maps suitable for inspection, localization, and downstream autonomy. The emphasis is on clear stages (preprocessing, odometry, export) so results can be reproduced and compared across runs.

Fundamentals: how the mapping stack works

Scan-to-map LiDAR odometry. Each new scan is aligned to a growing local map rather than only to the previous scan. That tends to stabilize drift and improves robustness in structured environments where consecutive frames can be partially degenerate.

GICP-class registration. MOLA is configured with a generalized-ICP-style pipeline for point-cloud alignment: the optimizer exploits local surface structure so walls, objects, and vertical detail contribute meaningfully to the pose estimate.

Keyframes and map products. Selected poses and associated clouds are stored as a keyframe trajectory and a .simplemap artifact; a further step can produce a dense metric map (.mm) for visualization in MM Viewer or for integration with other tools.

Optional ground filtering. Removing dominant ground returns before odometry can reduce planar ambiguity (floors often dominate point counts) and can make above-ground structure easier to interpret—at the cost of losing explicit floor geometry in the displayed map.

Optional LiDAR–IMU fusion. When IMU data are available, they support motion modeling and point-cloud motion compensation during mapping, which can improve consistency under faster motion or when timing and distortion matter. Fusion is not a substitute for good geometry, but it complements scan-to-map alignment when tuned appropriately.

Two scenarios on the same site

To make trade-offs concrete, we ran two configurations in the same environment (see the paired figures and the two demo videos above). They share the same core idea— MOLA scan-to-map odometry on RoboSense data—but differ in preprocessing density and whether IMU is used.

Scenario A — Ground-filtered, downsampled LiDAR (no IMU in this path). Point clouds are downsampled and ground returns are suppressed before odometry. The resulting map tends to emphasize walls and objects with less floor clutter—useful for clean visualization and for registration that is less dominated by large horizontal planes.

Scenario B — Full-resolution LiDAR + IMU fusion, ground filtering disabled. The full point cloud is retained and IMU measurements are fused in the MOLA LiDAR–IMU pipeline. The reconstruction is typically denser and preserves ground and low-level structure, which is informative for completeness but can increase visual clutter compared with Scenario A.

Pipeline summary

  • Inputs: ROS 2 bag — RoboSense sensor_msgs/PointCloud2; Scenario B additionally uses sensor_msgs/Imu (e.g. /imu/data).
  • Preprocessing: Scenario A — downsampling + ground filtering; Scenario B — full resolution, no ground filter.
  • Odometry / mapping: MOLA — GICP-based scan-to-map LiDAR odometry; Scenario B — LiDAR–IMU fusion variant.
  • Outputs: trajectory and keyframe .simplemap; optional export to metric .mm for MM Viewer and downstream use.

Why it matters

Choosing preprocessing and sensor fusion is not cosmetic—it changes what the map looks like and how well registration behaves in real buildings. A documented, side-by-side workflow like this supports digital twins, field deployment of mobile robots, and honest communication with stakeholders about map fidelity versus interpretability.
